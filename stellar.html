Stellar Evolution

Life Cycle of a Star

The life cycle of a star is a dynamic process that depends largely on its initial mass. It begins in a stellar nursery, where a cloud of gas and dust—called a nebula—collapses under gravity to form a protostar. As the protostar heats up and nuclear fusion begins, it becomes a main-sequence star, where it spends most of its life converting hydrogen into helium. When the hydrogen fuel is depleted, the star expands into a red giant (or red supergiant for massive stars). Low to medium-mass stars eventually shed their outer layers, forming a planetary nebula, and leave behind a dense core known as a white dwarf. In contrast, massive stars undergo a supernova explosion, leaving behind either a neutron star or a black hole. This stellar evolution process recycles elements into space, contributing to the formation of new stars and planets.

Variable Stars

Variable stars are stars whose brightness, as seen from Earth, changes over time. These variations in brightness can occur due to intrinsic factors, such as changes in the star’s size, temperature, or internal processes, or extrinsic factors, like eclipses in binary star systems where one star passes in front of the other. Intrinsic variable stars include pulsating stars like Cepheids and RR Lyrae, which expand and contract in a regular cycle, making them valuable tools for measuring cosmic distances. Eclipsing binaries and rotating stars with dark spots are examples of extrinsic variables. Studying variable stars helps astronomers understand stellar structure, evolution, and even the scale of the universe.
