More About Our Solar System

The Sun

The Sun is a G-type main-sequence star (G2V)and the center of our solar system, around 4.6 billion years old. It is approximately 1.39 million km in diameter and composed mostly of hydrogen (74%) and helium (24%), with nuclear fusion in its 15-million-degree Celsius coreconverting hydrogen into helium and releasing energy. This energy moves through the radiative and convective zones before reaching the photosphere, the Sun’s visible surface. Surrounding layers include the chromosphere and the corona, which extends millions of kilometers into space, emitting solar wind. The Sun undergoes an 11-year solar cycle, marked by sunspots, solar flares, and coronal mass ejections, which can impact Earth's magnetosphere, causing auroras and occasionally disrupting communications. As the primary source of energy, the Sun drives Earth's climate, supports photosynthesis, and sustains life. In about 5 billion years, it will expand into a red giant, shedding its outer layers before becoming a white dwarf. Despite its constant activity, the Sun remains the vital force maintaining the balance of our solar system. ☀

Planets

  1. Mercury

    Mercury is the smallest and closest planet to the Sun, orbiting at an average distance of 57.9 million km. It has a thin atmosphere mostly composed of oxygen, sodium, and hydrogen, making it incapable of retaining heat. As a result, temperatures range from 430°C (800°F) during the day to -180°C (-290°F) at night. Mercury has a heavily cratered surface similar to the Moon and lacks any significant geological activity. It has the fastest orbit around the Sun, completing a revolution in just 88 days, but it rotates very slowly, taking 59 Earth days to complete one rotation. Due to its proximity to the Sun and lack of an atmosphere, it experiences extreme temperature fluctuations.

  2. Venus

    Often called Earth’s twin due to its similar size and composition, Venus is actually the hottest planet in the solar system with an average surface temperature of 467°C (872°F). Its thick atmosphere is composed mostly of carbon dioxide with clouds of sulfuric acid, creating a strong greenhouse effect that traps heat. Venus has a very slow and retrograde rotation, meaning it spins backward compared to most planets, taking 243 Earth days to rotate once but only 225 days to complete an orbit around the Sun. The planet’s surface is covered in volcanoes, mountains, and vast plains, with evidence of past volcanic activity. Due to its extreme conditions, Venus is one of the most hostile planets in the solar system.

  3. Earth

    Earth is the only planet known to support life, with an atmosphere composed of 78% nitrogen, 21% oxygen, and trace gases that regulate temperature. It has a stable climate, liquid water covering 71% of its surface, and a protective magnetosphere that shields it from harmful solar radiation. Earth orbits the Sun at an average distance of 149.6 million km and completes one revolution in 365.25 days while rotating every 24 hours. The planet has diverse ecosystems, ranging from deserts to rainforests, and is home to over 8.7 million species. Earth's natural satellite, the Moon, affects tides and stabilizes its axial tilt, which is responsible for seasons.

  4. Mars

    Known as the Red Planet due to its iron-rich soil, Mars is the second smallest planet and orbits the Sun at an average distance of 227.9 million km. It has a thin atmosphere of carbon dioxide, making it incapable of supporting liquid water on the surface, though polar ice caps and evidence of ancient rivers suggest it once had a wetter climate. Mars has the largest volcano in the solar system, Olympus Mons, and the deepest canyon, Valles Marineris. It takes 687 Earth days to orbit the Sun and 24.6 hours to complete a rotation, making its day length similar to Earth's. Mars is the primary focus of space exploration, with multiple rovers and planned missions for potential future colonization.

  5. Jupiter

    Jupiter is the largest planet in the solar system, with a mass over 318 times that of Earth and a thick atmosphere composed mainly of hydrogen and helium. It is known for the Great Red Spot, a massive storm that has raged for centuries. Jupiter has a strong magnetic field and at least 95 known moons, including Ganymede , the largest moon in the solar system. The planet completes a fast rotation in just 10 hours, giving it a flattened shape, while its orbit around the Sun takes 12 Earth years . Jupiter has no solid surface, as it is a gas giant, but its core is believed to be rocky or metallic. Its powerful gravity helps shield the inner planets from asteroid impacts.

  6. Saturn

    Saturn is famous for its magnificent ring system, composed of ice and rock particles. It is the second-largest planet in the solar system and, like Jupiter, is a gas giant primarily made of hydrogen and helium. Saturn’s density is so low that it could float on water if a large enough body existed. It has over 146 moons , with Titan, its largest moon, being one of the most Earth-like bodies in the solar system due to its dense atmosphere and liquid methane lakes. Saturn takes 29.5 Earth years to complete an orbit and rotates rapidly, completing a day in just 10.7 hours. The planet's winds can reach speeds of ,800 km/h (1,118 mph), making it one of the windiest places in the solar system.

  7. Uranus

    Uranus is an ice giant with a unique tilt of 98 degrees, meaning it essentially rolls on its side as it orbits the Sun. This extreme tilt causes 42-year-long seasons , where one hemisphere experiences continuous daylight while the other is in darkness. It has a pale blue-green color due to methane in its atmosphere, which absorbs red light. Uranus is mostly composed of water, ammonia, and methane ice, and its atmosphere contains some of the coldest temperatures in the solar system, reaching -224°C (-371°F) . It has 13 faint rings and 27 known moons, with Miranda being one of the most geologically active. Uranus takes 84 Earth years to complete an orbit and rotates in 17.2 hours.

  8. Neptune

    Neptune, the farthest planet from the Sun, is also an ice giant like Uranus but appears deep blue due to higher methane concentrations. It has the strongest winds in the solar system, reaching speeds of 2,100 km/h (1,300 mph). Neptune orbits the Sun at an average distance of 4.5 billion km, taking 165 Earth years to complete one revolution. Its largest moon, Triton, orbits backward, suggesting it was captured from the Kuiper Belt. The planet has six faint rings and 14 known moons. Due to its distance, Neptune receives very little sunlight, and its interior heat contributes significantly to its weather patterns, including massive storms like the Great Dark Spot.
